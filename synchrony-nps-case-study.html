This project started with a single mandate: replace an external consulting firm's NPS model with something we own internally. What followed was a full-stack data science engagement — part infrastructure rebuild, part statistical investigation, part causal inference — that ended with a decision by analytics leadership to redesign the survey instrument itself based on what the model found.

Context

Why the Vendor Model Had to Go

Synchrony was paying a third-party consulting firm to deliver a black-box NPS driver model on a recurring basis. The outputs were used to understand which survey questions most influenced customer satisfaction — but the approach had three compounding problems:

No Internal Ownership
The model lived entirely with the vendor. Every methodology question required a consultant call. There was no internal ability to re-run, audit, or adapt the model to new survey designs — making iterative product improvement nearly impossible.
SAS-Locked Infrastructure
All data prep lived in legacy SAS/SQL workflows — slow, brittle, and requiring specialist knowledge to maintain. The ETL pipeline took over 2 hours to run, and schema mismatches between the 11 survey datasets caused silent deduplication failures that went undetected.
Unknown Data Quality
No one had done a systematic analysis of NPS distribution or survey coverage quality. The existing model inherited whatever problems were in the raw data — including 90% sparse coverage at the account level, which we later discovered was invalidating the account-level modeling granularity.
"Before I could model anything, I had to figure out whether the data was even modelable. That turned out to be the harder problem."
Phase 1 · Infrastructure

Rebuilding the Data Foundation

The pipeline ingests and joins 11 distinct survey datasets with account-level transaction records — each survey with its own schema, naming conventions, and coverage patterns. Key engineering challenges:

Schema Mismatch Resolution
Different surveys encoded the same NPS question with different field names, data types, and response scales. I built a schema normalization layer that mapped each survey's question format to a canonical representation — catching ~6 variants of "question ID" fields alone.
Deduplication at Scale
The original SAS pipeline had a latent deduplication bug — accounts with multiple survey responses in the same window were being counted multiple times. Fixing this required account-level deduplication logic before any aggregation step, which changed the effective dataset size.
Modular, Reproducible Design
Built as a modular Python pipeline: separate stages for ingestion, normalization, join, deduplication, and feature assembly. Each stage writes a checkpoint, making it easy to debug a single stage without re-running the full 100M+ row pipeline.

Phase 2 · Discovery

What the Data Was Actually Telling Us

With a clean pipeline in place, I ran systematic univariate and multivariate analysis across all 10+ surveys. Two findings immediately changed the modeling strategy.

Finding 1: Heavily Skewed NPS Targets
NPS scores across most surveys were severely right-skewed — the majority of responses clustered at 9 and 10, with very few detractors (0–6). This matters because standard regression on skewed targets inflates apparent fit. Any model claiming high R² on this distribution needed to be treated with suspicion. This finding informed our choice to log-transform the NPS target before modeling and to evaluate MAE in original score space rather than RMSE.
Finding 2: 90% Sparse Coverage at Account Level
Most accounts appeared in only 1 of the 11 surveys — 90% sparse coverage meant that account-level modeling would require imputing or ignoring 90% of features for any given account. This is a fatal signal sparsity problem. The prior vendor model had been running at account level — meaning it was effectively modeling noise. We redesigned the modeling granularity to the survey level, where coverage was complete within each survey's own respondent population.
Redesigning the Modeling Granularity
Instead of asking "what drives NPS across all accounts?", we reframed to "what drives NPS within a specific survey population?" — a more tractable and honest question. We selected the Apply Experience Survey as the primary modeling target: highest response volume, richest question set, and most actionable for the product team.
Why This Matters
  • The account-level model had a flawed premise. With 90% sparsity, cross-survey feature imputation would have introduced more noise than signal. Our redesign prevented a model that looked good on paper but would have misled leadership.
  • Survey-level granularity enables actionable findings. When we identify question X as a top NPS driver within the Apply Experience Survey, the product team can act on it directly — which is exactly what happened.
  • Distribution analysis should gate modeling. Finding the NPS skew before running models saved weeks of debugging spurious results downstream.
Phase 3 · Modeling

Running Two Models in Parallel, on Purpose

We modeled NPS drivers at the Apply Experience Survey level using two approaches simultaneously: XGBoost for predictive accuracy and Lasso regression as a cross-validation signal. Running them in parallel wasn't redundancy — it was a deliberate interpretability strategy.

Approach Purpose Key Output Performance
XGBoost Predictive accuracy; captures non-linear interactions between survey questions Feature importance ranking R²/RMSE similar to Lasso — no overfitting
Lasso Regression Regularized linear model; coefficients directly interpretable as NPS driver strength Signed coefficient weights per survey question R² 0.43

The key insight: when XGBoost feature importances and Lasso coefficients agreed on the same questions, we had high confidence those were genuine NPS drivers, not model-specific artifacts. When they disagreed, we investigated the discrepancy rather than picking a winner. This cross-validation-by-architecture approach is more robust than running a single model and trusting its internal importance scores.

Phase 4 · Validation

Proving the Signal Is Real

Correlation between survey questions and NPS scores is expected — after all, they're measured together. The harder question is: do the questions we identified actually drive NPS, or did we find a statistical artifact? To answer this, I applied Placebo Refutation Testing from the causal inference literature.

Placebo Refutation Test — What It Does

A placebo refutation test works by replacing the actual treatment variable with a randomly generated placebo — a variable that by construction has no causal relationship with the outcome. If the estimated effect of the placebo is near zero, the original model's identified effect is likely genuine signal rather than spurious correlation from confounders or model structure.

−0.001 Estimated placebo effect (target: near zero)

The near-zero placebo effect (−0.001) is the test passing. It means: when we swap in a random variable for the survey driver, the model finds no effect — confirming that the effects we identified for real survey questions are not artifacts of model structure or shared confounders. The NPS drivers we found reflect genuine signal.

This is critical for the business use case. If analytics leadership is going to redesign the survey instrument based on which questions matter most, they need confidence that the identified drivers aren't statistical noise. Placebo refutation provides that confidence in a way that standard metrics like R² cannot.

Why Causal Validation Before Survey Redesign
  • Survey redesigns have high organizational cost — removing questions that stakeholders have tracked for years requires strong justification.
  • Predictive performance alone is not sufficient. A model with R² 0.43 explains substantial variance, but the relevant question for survey redesign is whether the identified questions have genuine causal standing.
  • Placebo refutation is a falsification test — it can disprove the hypothesis that we found real drivers. The near-zero result means we could not falsify it, strengthening confidence in the findings.

Phase 5 · Impact

What Changed as a Result

Business Outcomes

  • Eliminated the external consulting firm's NPS model — replaced with a fully internal, reproducible solution that the team can re-run, adapt, and audit without external dependency.
  • Survey redesign initiated by customer analytics leadership — findings on top NPS-driving questions were adopted directly, with low-signal questions identified for removal from future survey waves.
  • 87% reduction in pipeline runtime enables the team to iterate on modeling assumptions and feature definitions in hours rather than days.
  • Corrected a structural data error — the account-level modeling flaw (90% sparsity) that had been silently running in the prior vendor model was identified and fixed through redesign to survey-level granularity.

The most consequential output wasn't a number — it was a change in how Synchrony thinks about the survey itself. When the causal analysis showed that certain questions had near-zero driver strength while others dominated NPS movement, that became the basis for a structural redesign of the survey instrument. The questions that didn't move the needle are candidates for removal; the questions that did are candidates for expansion and deeper follow-up.

That's the full loop: better infrastructure → honest EDA → survey-level modeling → causal validation → actionable survey redesign. Each step made the next one possible, and the final output is a recommendation the business can actually use.
